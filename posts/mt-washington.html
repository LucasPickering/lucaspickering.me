Cogs and Cairns: A Hike Up Mount Washington

Jul 28, 2021

At 6,288 feet, Mount Washington is New England's tallest mountain. It also bears some of the harshest weather in the world (read on if you don't believe me). Most unique of all though, there are three different ways to reach the top of the mountain: by foot, by car, and by train.

This is my story of climbing Mount Washington. There are many like it, but this one is mine.

Ammonoosuc Ravine Trail

Onward and upward.

Verdant undergrowth covers the base of the mountain.

Thousands of hikers climb the mountain on a sunny summer day.

Stream crossings are common in the first few miles of the trail.

A waterfall provides a convenient excuse for us to catch our breaths.

Lakes of the Clouds

Once you breach the treeline, hundreds of cairns guide the way. A necessity when the weather it at its worst.

The first glimpses of the mountain's apex. The weather station at the summit is visible from miles away.

A lone cairn on a cliff's edge.

The view back to the hut, about halfway from the lakes to the summit.

The rough stones provide a sort of lunar backdrop to the final leg of the climb.

The Tuckerman trail crosses the alpine moonscape.

Leading the way.
